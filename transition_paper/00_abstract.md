# Abstract

How resilient are ethnic-political boundaries within unified communities? This paper exploits a natural experiment—Israel's 2020-21 electoral crisis—to examine voter transitions within the ultra-Orthodox (Haredi) population, where Ashkenazi and Sephardic subgroups maintain near-total political separation despite shared religious practice. Using ecological inference on ballot-box data from five Haredi population hubs across six elections (2019-2022), I document exceptionally high baseline party loyalty (90-95%), a dramatic but temporary disruption during the March 2020–March 2021 transition when cross-ethnic switching surged to 12-19%, and rapid recovery to pre-crisis levels within 13 months.

The findings challenge conventional interpretations of electoral volatility in cohesive communities. The sharp, synchronized disruption and recovery across geographically dispersed cities—occurring without residential mobility—reveals extreme institutional discipline rather than emerging voter independence. This pattern suggests coordinated elite guidance (likely rabbinic authorities orchestrating strategic electoral shifts) capable of executing system-wide reversible pivots under stress. The paradox of synchronized change demonstrates stronger, not weaker, communal cohesion.

Beyond substantive contributions to understanding identity-based voting and boundary maintenance, the study exposes a critical methodological flaw: using voting patterns to proxy residential segregation assumes stable political loyalty. When voters switched parties without moving residences, dissimilarity indices falsely registered political realignment as spatial integration—a confounding that threatens any study conflating demographic and political boundaries. The combination of transition matrices with segregation indices offers a dynamic framework applicable to immigrant communities, religious denominations, and linguistic minorities worldwide.

The analysis reveals that ethnic-political boundaries are simultaneously rigid and vulnerable: deeply entrenched under normal conditions yet capable of temporary disruption under sufficient stress, followed by rapid reconstitution. This pattern of punctuated volatility within disciplined identity blocs has implications for understanding electoral behavior in contexts ranging from U.S. evangelical communities to Catholic voters in Latin America to ethnic-minority parties in Eastern Europe—wherever strong institutional structures mediate between individual preferences and collective political expression.
