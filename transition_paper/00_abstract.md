# Abstract

Israel's ultra-Orthodox (Haredi) electorate is widely regarded as one of the most disciplined voting blocs in democratic
politics, with community members consistently supporting sectoral parties under rabbinic guidance. Yet beneath this
outward stability, can hidden volatility exist? This study analyzes voter transitions within the Haredi sector across
successive Knesset elections from January 2013 through November 2022, using hierarchical Bayesian ecological inference on
polling-station data restricted to ballot boxes with at least 75% Haredi composition. The analysis focuses on four
categories: Shas (the Sephardic ultra-Orthodox party), United Torah Judaism (UTJ, the Ashkenazi ultra-Orthodox alliance),
other parties, and abstention.

Three key findings emerge. First, under normal conditions both Haredi parties demonstrate exceptionally high voter
retention rates exceeding 95%, confirming the conventional portrait of bloc discipline. However, the March 2020–March
2021 transition (Knesset 23→24) revealed a dramatic temporary disruption: Shas-to-Shas loyalty plummeted to 73.5%
nationally and 67.1% in Ashdod, while Shas-to-UTJ switching surged to 12.3% nationally and 19.3% in Ashdod. This
represents an unprecedented cross-ethnic flow within the Haredi electorate. Critically, loyalty patterns recovered fully
by the subsequent March 2021–November 2022 transition, indicating temporary disruption rather than permanent realignment.

Second, while this loyalty collapse affected all major Haredi population centers, its magnitude varied substantially
across cities. Ashdod exhibited the most extreme deviation, with switching rates 57% higher than the national average.
This geographic variation suggests that local demographic integration modulates the intensity of electoral volatility even
during system-wide shocks.

Third, this voter transition pattern resolves an anomaly from previous research: Ashdod's unexplained drop in residential
segregation indices during the same period. The 19.3% cross-party switching created a temporary mixing of voting
patterns that manifested as apparent residential integration without any physical population movement. This reveals a
fundamental methodological limitation: using party votes to proxy ethnic residential segregation assumes stable political
loyalty, an assumption that breaks down during electoral disruptions.
